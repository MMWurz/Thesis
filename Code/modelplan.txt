 Adopt an existing model — don't write from scratch

  The short answer: There are well-established supply chain network flow models in the critical raw materials (CRM) and
  energy materials literature that you can adopt as your structural base, then extend with your three objectives.
  Writing from scratch would be academically weaker and take much longer without adding novelty.

  ---
  What exists that you can build on

  Supply chain model structure to adopt:
  A multi-echelon network flow model (MILP/LP) is the standard. Nodes = supply sources, processing facilities,
  end-users. Edges = material flows with associated cost, emission, and risk factors. This structure is well-established
  for:

  - Battery lithium supply chains (very close analog — same material, different isotope fraction)
  - EU critical raw material supply chains (JRC/EC reports use these for REEs, cobalt, lithium)
  - Rare earth element (REE) supply chains under supply risk

  Key methodological sources to look for:
  - Helbig, Wietschel et al. — supply risk indicators for CRMs (HHI, WGI-based)
  - Olivetti, Ceder et al. on lithium supply chain constraints (Joule, 2017)
  - EU JRC Critical Raw Materials reports — they formalize supply risk metrics directly relevant to your "EU supply
  autonomy" objective
  - ε-constraint method papers (Haimes, 1971 origin) — standard for converting single-objective MILP to MOO without
  changing the model structure

  ---
  What your thesis contributes (the novel part)

  The novelty is NOT the model structure — it's:
  1. First application to Li-6 (isotope-separated material, not commodity Li — very different supply chain with
  enrichment steps)
  2. The three-objective MOO combination (cost + GWP + EU autonomy together)
  3. Fusion-specific demand modeling (tritium breeding blanket requirements per GW)

  That framing is academically defensible and appropriately scoped for a master's thesis.

  ---
  Recommended approach

  1. Take a CRM network-flow MILP from literature (pick one paper as your "base model")
  2. Add your three objective functions on top of that structure
  3. Use ε-constraint method (easiest to implement, interpretable Pareto front, standard in OR literature) to generate
  trade-off curves
  4. Implement in Python with Pyomo or PuLP

  The ε-constraint approach lets you run the model repeatedly, fixing one or two objectives as constraints and
  minimizing the third — you get a Pareto front without changing your model formulation fundamentally.
